# What is a crack spread and why do oil investors monitor it?

A crack spread is the price difference between crude oil and the refined petroleum products (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel) produced from it. The term comes from the refining process of "cracking" crude oil molecules into lighter products. The most common benchmark is the 3-2-1 crack spread: the profit from refining 3 barrels of crude into 2 barrels of gasoline and 1 barrel of distillate (diesel/heating oil). Investors monitor crack spreads because they indicate refinery profitability — when spreads are wide, refiners earn more per barrel processed. Narrow or negative spreads signal margin compression and potential refinery shutdowns. For upstream investors, crack spreads affect demand for crude: weak refining margins reduce crude purchases. For portfolio managers, crack spread trends are leading indicators of energy sector earnings. Monitor the 3-2-1 spread on CME Group or EIA data. A spread above $20/barrel is historically strong; below $10 signals stress. Seasonal patterns matter — spreads typically widen before summer driving season (gasoline demand) and winter heating season (distillate demand).
