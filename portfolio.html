Pedagogical judgment, encoded in code

OKA is a learning companion for an 8-year-old with dyslexia (98th percentile verbal reasoning, 1st percentile word reading). The interface uses a reading-mode state machine with word bands, adaptive difficulty, frustration detection, a hint ladder (try first → one hint → second try → calm answer → simplify), and streak-based band progression. The design is warm, voice-first, and audience-aware — rounded corners, CSS-only avatar, calm color palette. Built with a real child. Tested with a real child.

Learning Architecture

  • Reading-mode state machine with 6 word bands
  • Adaptive difficulty: 3 correct → advance, 2 struggles → drop
  • Frustration detection + automatic simplification
  • Trouble pattern tracking per phonological focus
  • Streak-based confidence signaling

Design Decisions

  • Warm peach/cream palette for emotional safety
  • CSS-only avatar (no external assets)
  • Voice button with 3 states (idle/listening/thinking)
  • Purple for decodable words, red for irregular
  • One question at a time — never stack demands

Technical Stack

  • 896 lines HTML/CSS/JS (frontend)
  • 2,573 lines Node.js (server + state machine)
  • 190-line system prompt (coaching personality)
  • Web Speech API for voice input
  • OpenAI GPT for conversational responses

Design Philosophy
How I think about learning systems

Architecture over content

A workshop becomes a 3-minute Slack insight backed by an AI coaching interface. The insight, not the format, is what scales. I design the knowledge structures that surface the right thing at the right moment.

Invisible learning

At Ecole des Metiers du Livre, students learned English through a simulated publishing process. They never realized they were studying language. The best learning design disappears — the system does the teaching.

Measure behavior, not completion

"Did they finish the course?" is the wrong question. "Did their work change?" is the right one. At AWS I built adoption instrumentation tracking behavior change and competency progression — not vanity metrics.

Builder of builders

I build the playbooks, templates, coaching systems, and assessment rubrics that let your team deliver great learning without me in the room. The Carabinieri trainer didn't need linguistics theory — just the scaffolding I designed. That's the pattern: decouple the expertise from the expert.

Confidence before skill

OKA never starts with reading practice — it starts with something Nora is good at. The Carabinieri never opened textbooks — they read The Economist. When people feel capable, the skill work becomes possible.

Design in code

I don't mock in Figma and hand off — I build the design system in CSS, the interaction model in TypeScript, the coaching logic in the state machine. The design judgment IS the code. That's what "thinks like a product engineer" means.

If I Were Starting Monday
How I'd approach a company's learning architecture

Week 1–2: Audit

Walk every flagship program. Where is the rigor? What makes it work when it works? What's the insight people leave with? Map the content supply chain: who creates, who maintains, who delivers, who measures.

Week 3–4: Classify

Some elements are inherently high-touch — they need the room, the facilitator, the vulnerability. Those stay. But the knowledge transfer underneath? That can be architectured into documentation, internal tools, and AI coaching interfaces.

Week 5–8: Build the infrastructure

Standardized templates. An AI prompt library grounded in the company's operating principles. Knowledge structures that surface at the moment of need. The 2-hour workshop doesn't become a 2-hour video — it becomes a 3-minute insight backed by depth when asked.

Ongoing: Measure behavior

Did their work change? Not: did they finish the module. Build adoption instrumentation tracking behavior change and competency progression — the same infrastructure I built at AWS for 12,000+ users.
